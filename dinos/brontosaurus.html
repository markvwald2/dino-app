What's in a Name?

If the name Brontosaurus sounds unfamiliar, that's because it is now known as Apatasaurus. When paleontologists first discovered the bones of this giant herbivore in 1877, they named it Apatosaurus. Years later, they found larger, but similar, bones, and called the supposedly new dinosaur Brontosaurus. It turned out that what they thought were Brontosaurus bones were actually grown-up Apatosaurus bones, and scientists went back to the original name.

Say My Name:

uh-PAT-oh-SORE-us

Standing Eater

Because its back legs were slightly longer than its front legs, some paleontologists think Apatosaurus reared up to nibble on the tops of trees.



Not a Chewer

Apatosaurus would keep rocks in its stomach, so that it could swallow vegetation whole. The rocks would grind up the food instead of the Apatosaurus having to chew it.
